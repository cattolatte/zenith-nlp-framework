The observatory sat on the ridge above the town, and every night the keeper
climbed the narrow stair to watch the sky. He kept a journal of what he saw,
and the journal grew thick with the names of stars and the paths they traced.

On clear nights the keeper opened the great dome and let the cold air pour in.
The telescope turned slowly on its mount, and the gears made a low steady sound
that he had come to love. He wrote down the hour, the weather, and the quiet.

People in the town below did not often think about the sky. They worked and
slept and rose again, and the stars turned above them whether they looked or
not. But the keeper looked, and in looking he felt that the night belonged to
him, or that he belonged to the night, which was closer to the truth.

Once a child came up the ridge to ask what the keeper was looking for. The
keeper thought for a while and said that he was not looking for anything in
particular, only paying attention, and that attention was its own reward. The
child did not understand, but she remembered the answer for many years.

In winter the snow lay deep on the ridge and the stair was hard to climb. The
keeper climbed it anyway, because the winter sky was the clearest of all, and
the cold made the stars burn sharp and steady over the sleeping town below.

He grew old on the ridge, and his journals filled a long shelf in the dome.
When he could no longer climb the stair, a young woman came to take his place.
He gave her the journals and told her only to pay attention, and to write down
what she saw, and to keep the dome open on the clear cold nights of winter.

She did. And the town below worked and slept and rose again, and the stars
turned above them, and someone on the ridge was always watching, and writing,
and paying quiet attention to the turning of the night.
